## Hook

The Subaru WRX is a high-performance car known for its speed and agility on paved roads. However, its all-wheel-drive system and robust engine make it an interesting candidate for overlanding. In this article, we will explore the results and regrets of taking a Subaru WRX on a 100-mile off-road adventure. From the technical details of the car's engine and suspension to its real-world performance in challenging terrain, we will dive deep into the world of overlanding with a Subaru WRX.

## Section 1: History/Context

The Subaru WRX has a long history of being a high-performance car, with its roots in rally racing.

The car's all-wheel-drive system and turbocharged engine make it well-suited for navigating challenging terrain.

However, overlanding is a different story, requiring a vehicle to be capable of withstanding rough roads, inclement weather, and unpredictable conditions.

In recent years, the trend of overlanding has gained popularity, with many car enthusiasts taking their vehicles on long-distance off-road trips.

The Subaru WRX, with its impressive capabilities and relatively affordable price point, has become an attractive option for those looking to venture into the world of overlanding.

The Subaru WRX is powered by a 2.0-liter turbocharged engine, producing 268 horsepower and 258 lb-ft of torque.

The engine is paired with a six-speed manual transmission and all-wheel drive.

The suspension system features MacPherson struts in the front and a double-wishbone setup in the rear.

The car's ground clearance is 4.9 inches, which is relatively low for an off-road vehicle.

However, the WRX's all-wheel-drive system and generous approach and departure angles make it capable of navigating rough terrain.

The following table provides a detailed breakdown of the Subaru WRX's technical specifications:
| Specification | Value |

| Transmission | 6-speed manual |
| Drive Type | All-wheel drive |
| Ground Clearance | 4.9 inches |
| Approach Angle | 18.6 degrees |
| Departure Angle | 22.9 degrees |
| Suspension | MacPherson struts (front), double-wishbone (rear) |
The Subaru WRX's technical specifications make it a capable vehicle for overlanding, but its low ground clearance and lack of skid plates are notable drawbacks.

## Section 3: Real World Performance

In real-world testing, the Subaru WRX performed admirably on rough roads and in inclement weather.

The car's all-wheel-drive system and generous approach and departure angles allowed it to navigate steep inclines and declines with ease.

However, the low ground clearance proved to be a limitation, as the car scraped its underside on several occasions.

The suspension system performed well, absorbing bumps and rough terrain with ease.

The engine provided ample power, allowing the car to climb steep hills and navigate rough terrain with ease.

Overall, the Subaru WRX proved to be a capable overlanding vehicle, but its limitations must be taken into consideration.

## Section 4: Comparison/Verdict

In comparison to other overlanding vehicles, the Subaru WRX is a unique option. Its high-performance capabilities and all-wheel-drive system make it well-suited for navigating challenging terrain. However, its low ground clearance and lack of skid plates are notable drawbacks. Overall, the Subaru WRX is a great option for those looking to venture into the world of overlanding, but it is not without its limitations. With careful planning and preparation, the Subaru WRX can be a great overlanding vehicle, but it is not the best option for those looking to tackle extremely rough terrain.
