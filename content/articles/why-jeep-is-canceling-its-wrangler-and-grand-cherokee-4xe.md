## Hook

The automotive world has been abuzz with the recent announcement that Jeep is canceling its Wrangler and Grand Cherokee 4xe powertrains. This decision has left many enthusiasts and potential buyers wondering about the future of electric Jeeps. As a senior automotive journalist, I will delve into the history and context behind this decision, explore the technical details of the affected powertrains, examine real-world performance, and compare the 4xe powertrains to their competitors.

## Section 1: History/Context

The Jeep Wrangler and Grand Cherokee are two of the most iconic vehicles in the Jeep lineup, with a rich history dating back to the 1940s.

Over the years, these vehicles have undergone numerous transformations, with a focus on improving performance, capability, and sustainability.

The introduction of the 4xe powertrain was a significant milestone in this journey, marking Jeep's foray into the world of electric vehicles.

However, despite the initial excitement, the 4xe powertrains have not lived up to expectations, with concerns over range, charging time, and overall performance.

## Section 2: Technical Details/Engine/Suspension

The Wrangler and Grand Cherokee 4xe powertrains were designed to provide a unique blend of electric and gasoline power.

The powertrains consisted of a 2.0-liter turbocharged inline-four cylinder engine, paired with an electric motor and a 17-kilowatt-hour battery pack.

This combination provided an estimated 375 horsepower and 470 lb-ft of torque, with an electric-only range of up to 25 miles.

However, the complexity of the powertrain and the weight of the battery pack took a toll on the vehicles' overall performance, with many owners reporting decreased towing capacity, reduced ground clearance, and increased maintenance costs.

## Section 3: Real World Performance

In real-world testing, the Wrangler and Grand Cherokee 4xe powertrains have shown promise, but have ultimately fallen short of expectations. The electric-only mode provides a smooth and quiet driving experience, but the transition to gasoline power can be jerky and unpredictable. Additionally, the weight of the battery pack has affected the vehicles' off-road capability, with reduced ground clearance and decreased approach and departure angles. While the 4xe powertrains have their strengths, they have not lived up to the standards set by their gasoline-powered counterparts.

## Section 4: Comparison/Verdict

The cancellation of the Wrangler and Grand Cherokee 4xe powertrains is a significant setback for Jeep's electric vehicle ambitions.

However, it also presents an opportunity for the company to regroup and focus on more viable solutions.

In comparison to other electric vehicles on the market, the 4xe powertrains were unique, but ultimately flawed.

As the automotive industry continues to evolve, it is likely that we will see more efficient and capable electric powertrains emerge.

For now, the future of electric Jeeps remains uncertain, but one thing is clear: the company will need to innovate and adapt to stay competitive in the ever-changing landscape of electric vehicles.
