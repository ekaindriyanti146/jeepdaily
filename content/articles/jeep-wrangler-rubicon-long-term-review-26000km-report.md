## Hook

The Jeep Wrangler Rubicon is an iconic off-road vehicle that has been a favorite among adventure-seekers for decades. With its rugged design, impressive ground clearance, and advanced four-wheel-drive system, it's no wonder why this vehicle has become a staple in the off-road community. But how does it hold up in the long term? In this review, we'll take a closer look at the Jeep Wrangler Rubicon after 26000km of driving, exploring its history, technical details, real-world performance, and how it compares to other models in its class.

## Section 1: History/Context

The Jeep Wrangler has a rich history that dates back to the 1940s, when the first Willys Jeep was introduced.

Over the years, the Wrangler has undergone numerous transformations, with each new generation bringing improvements in design, technology, and performance.

The Rubicon trim, in particular, was introduced in 2003, and it's designed to be the most off-road-capable Wrangler of them all.

With its Rock-Trac four-wheel-drive system, electronic locking differentials, and 33-inch all-terrain tires, the Rubicon is built to tackle even the toughest trails.

In this review, we'll explore how the Rubicon has held up over 26000km of driving, and whether it still lives up to its reputation as a top-notch off-road vehicle.

## Section 2: Technical Details/Engine/Suspension

The Jeep Wrangler Rubicon is powered by a 3.6-liter V6 engine that produces 285 horsepower and 260 lb-ft of torque.

It's paired with a six-speed manual transmission, although an eight-speed automatic is also available.

The Rubicon also features a range of advanced off-road technologies, including the Rock-Trac four-wheel-drive system, electronic locking differentials, and a disconnecting front sway bar.

The suspension is also designed to be highly capable, with a five-link coil suspension in the front and a five-link leaf spring suspension in the rear.

The following table provides a detailed overview of the Rubicon's technical specifications:

| Specification | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Engine | 3.6-liter V6 |
| Horsepower | 285 |
| Torque | 260 lb-ft |
| Transmission | Six-speed manual or eight-speed automatic |
| Four-wheel-drive system | Rock-Trac |
| Suspension | Five-link coil (front), five-link leaf spring (rear) |
| Ground clearance | 10.8 inches |
| Approach angle | 35 degrees |
| Departure angle | 29 degrees |
| Breakover angle | 22 degrees |

## Section 3: Real World Performance

So how does the Jeep Wrangler Rubicon perform in the real world? Over 26000km of driving, we've had the chance to put the Rubicon through its paces, tackling everything from rocky trails to sandy dunes.

The results have been impressive, with the Rubicon proving to be highly capable and reliable.

The Rock-Trac four-wheel-drive system has performed flawlessly, providing excellent traction and control on even the most challenging terrain.

The suspension has also been impressive, soaking up bumps and rough terrain with ease.

One of the few criticisms we have is that the Rubicon can be a bit rough on the road, with a stiff ride and noticeable wind noise at high speeds.

However, for those who plan to use their Rubicon primarily for off-road adventures, this is a small price to pay.

## Section 4: Comparison/Verdict

So how does the Jeep Wrangler Rubicon compare to other models in its class? The main competitors to the Rubicon are the Toyota 4Runner TRD Pro and the Land Rover Defender.

While both of these vehicles are highly capable off-road, they can't match the Rubicon's level of sophistication and refinement.

The 4Runner TRD Pro, in particular, feels a bit dated compared to the Rubicon, with a less advanced four-wheel-drive system and a less comfortable interior.

The Defender, on the other hand, is a more luxurious and refined vehicle, but it's also significantly more expensive than the Rubicon.

Overall, the Jeep Wrangler Rubicon is an excellent choice for those who want a highly capable off-road vehicle that's also comfortable and refined on the road.
